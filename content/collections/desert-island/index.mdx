In her 2019 book *Where Histories Reside*, film historian Priya Jaikumar argues for a spatial film historiography, using the term “filmed space.” Rather than looking at the profilmic setting as a narrative element, she argues for considering cinema to be creating a new sense of spatiality. Filmed space here is different from diegetic space, which refers to the space or the environment that shapes the narrative world of the film; instead, it refers to the space that throughout and after production and reception of the film continues to be impacted by the film. To identify a place as filmed space is to recognize how film transforms that space in material and imagined ways– filmed space persists beyond the film. 

> Retrieving a film’s background as a point of focus and analyzing it as a distinctive aesthetic and produce entity performs a few strategic inversions. It disarticulates the different stages of a film’s pre-production process prior to its manufacture as a unified textual and visual experience… It focuses on the ontological moment of the camera’s capture of the profilmic; it studies the ordering of the world within the film/s narrative and aesthetic schema; and it tracks the embodied and spatial experiences generated by that captured artifact of place in the afterlives of films as social objects.[^1]

I’d like to apply Jaikumar’s space-oriented film historiography to images of water by looking at them as representations and productions of watery spaces that exceed background portrayal — "Poetics of Aqueous Place," after Gaston Bachelard's "poetics of space."

One particular news story inspired me to examine how a filmed space becomes a filmed place and persists in the films’ afterlives, as Jaikumar says, and how this approach allows me to understand bodies of water as an environmentally precarious and politically dynamic space and a historied place imbued with memories and imagination at once.

On [April 4, 2026](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/apr/06/cruise-ship-grounded-reef-fiji-cast-away-island-monuriki) (less than two months ago from now), a sudden, violent gust of wind hit the cruise vessel Fiji Princess, carrying 30 passengers and 17 crew members, causing it to run aground off a reef of a remote Fijian island in the South Pacific. Everyone aboard the vessel evacuated safely without injury within the day. But if you really knew where they were, you might wonder if they might have been much more worried about their fate. 

In the year 2000, a man named Chuck Nolan was stranded on this very island for years, with nobody but a volleyball named Wilson. Of course, this is the massively successful movie Cast Away, <MapFocus title="Cast Away" /> and the man Chuck Nolan was a character played by Tom Hanks: a corporate man at Fedex survives a plane crash, alone, and finds himself on a desert island with no way out. In the real world, this is the Monuriki island, an uninhabited island in Fiji, in the South Pacific, where the parts of the film were shot. I think those of you who know the film can now imagine what this island is like, even if vaguely. It’s as if we, and probably those passengers, already know the island through the film, despite never having set foot. 

So what were the passengers of this cruise looking to find in this ocean?

### Ocean and the Other

Since the early voyages, the Pacific has been an exemplary site in Western intellectual traditions where Europe encountered its other. The early voyages were motivated by the search for the safest and quickest route to the Spice Islands and the hypothetical continent of *Terra Australis* (“southern land”), the first of which corresponds to modern-day Maluku in Indonesia – a prescient example of imaginary geography motivated by colonial pursuits. Eventually, these voyages came to mean much more than travel routes or territorial or economic gain. The voyagers’ encounters with Pacific Islanders, and the knowledge and artifacts they brought back to Europe in the 18th century, ended up making a significant impact on how Enlightenment thinkers constructed its own identity, of Europe, in contrast to its Black, Pacific, and Indigenous others. 

In the film *Cast Away*, the man is stranded on a desert island: here, he struggles to survive as the sole human in a completely uninhabited island. He slowly starts to establish a life for himself: using materials he finds on the island as resources and tools; creating a much-needed friend, in a volleyball he names Wilson. The character’s precarious balance between trying to maintain a semblance of his former life, and being accustomed to the new island environment, only ends up further emphasizing the oppositional logic between two approaches to the desert island life. 

![Poster of the 2000 film Cast Away, with the tagline: "At the edge of the world, his journey begins."](/collections/desert-island/cast-away-horiz.jpg)

Pacific studies scholar Alexander Mawyer describes this narrative in a nutshell as a “settler colonial fantasy of Oceania’s islands as *terra nullius*, empty lands that can be blank slates for the establishment of Euro-American cultural practices and an ethos of individual, personal development and self-fulfillment.” [^2] 

But the film goes one step further. In the film, the island is said to be "about 600 miles south of the Cook Islands": neither is this true about Monuriki Island, nor is there any land south of the Cook Islands other than Antarctica. The film doesn’t simply project a narrative onto a Pacific island— it literally does not exist! – it also, more profoundly, produces a Pacific Ocean that is geographically impossible. The original poster confirms the constructedness of the filmed space and the dream of *terra nullius*: "At the edge of the world, his journey begins."

This accident enjoyed major international media coverage: [The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/06/world/australia/castaway-cruise-shipwreck-fiji.html), The Guardian, [People.com](https://people.com/cruise-ship-hits-reef-near-cast-away-island-30-travelers-rescued-11943088), and [New York Post](https://nypost.com/2026/04/08/lifestyle/stranded-cruise-passengers-get-tom-hanks-treatment-on-cast-away-island/) reported on the accident and the island’s Cast Away fame. (Meanwhile [also in April](https://www.fijivillage.com/news/2-lives-lost-in-tragic-boat-collision-near-qamea-msaf-investigates-31ad95/), a tragic boat collision near Qamea, Fiji resulted in two deaths and no coverage in Western media outlets.) According to the Maritime Safety Authority of Fiji (MSAF), the removal of fuel was complete on [April 6](https://www.facebook.com/share/p/1BUxTKyikK/): approximately 23,000 liters of fuel was removed from the vessel; its last update on the accident on [April 14](https://www.facebook.com/share/p/18nVK54Aj8/) reads that the removal of the vessel is still underway. 

Cast Away Island (that is, not Monuriki Island, but the fictional island from *Cast Away*) is only one example of the archetypical space of the desert island generated and sustained by the film and its afterlife in the media and tourism industry. In fact, several seasons of the reality TV show *The Survivor* in fact were filmed on Monuriki Island and other small islands that make up the Mamanuca Islands, a Fijian volcanic archipelago in the South Pacific. 

### Looking for *The Beach* (2000)
<MapFocus title="The Beach (Maya Bay)" />

![Leonardo DiCaprio in front of the sea, in the film The Beach](/collections/desert-island/the-beach.jpg)

Interestingly, the example of the desert island does not need to be set in the literal Pacific Ocean to evoke the sense of a secluded paradise in the vast sea. Danny Boyle’s 2000 film *The Beach* famously features Maya Bay, a beach on the Thai island of Ko Phi Phi Leh in the Andaman Sea. While Maya Bay was chosen over many other alternatives because its features were deemed most fitting for the titular beach, the beautiful paradise that must be kept secret, mountains and other geographical features were added to the footage in post-production; the beach in the film is a carefully crafted construction. The filmmakers not only altered the filmed image of the island but physically engineered the look of the island to enhance its image. A [2022 Guardian article](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2022/sep/13/thai-court-orders-repair-of-the-beach-location-maya-bay-ko-phi-phi-leh-22-years-after-filming) outlines the controversy around the environmental impact of the shoot: “the filmmakers planted dozens of coconut trees to give a more ‘tropical’ feel to Maya Bay and were accused of ripping up vegetation growing on sand dunes.” 20th Century Fox claims otherwise; producer Andrew MacDonald noted that the crew in fact removed a lot of trash from the island and left it in good condition after the shoot.[^3] Regardless of the immediate physical impact of the film shoot, Maya Bay’s newfound Hollywood fame drew massive waves of tourists that became a much bigger concern. The beach was closed in 2018 to tourists, and was reopened in 2022; nowadays, only a limited number of visitors are allowed on the beach to help preserve the environment.

### *The Blue Lagoon*
<MapFocus title="Blue Lagoon" />

One final example for this series, although many more do surely exist, is *The Blue Lagoon* (1980), a story about two children shipwrecked on a tropical island and growing up together starring Brooke Shields and Chrostopher Atkins. Controversies abound in *The Blue Lagoon*: its use of nudity and taboo themes, the young age of its main actors (Brooke Shields was only 14).

More directly relevant to Aqueous Earth Catalog is the film's construction of the prototypical desert island. The film was mostly shot on the private island of Nanuya Levu (Turtle Island) in Fiji. Yet, other island locales have been incorrectly referenced as the setting for the film – sources claim the film was also shot in Jamaica and Vanuatu, both island nations in the Caribbean and the South Pacific, respectively, both associated with a lush tropical ecosystem. In other words, the constructed image of the paradise-like island forms an imaginary indexical relationship to places that share no real connection with the image, so much so that the locals in those places benefit from this non-existent, imaginary projection of the filmed space. 

Seen through this lens, it becomes clear that the film is another direct embodiment of the fantasy of the desert island and the colonial ethos of Euro-American self-fulfillment with the added layer of Victorian-era sexuality and gender roles. The film's protagonists idealize a sort of return to a utopic past, a more natural state of life; yet, their growth into adulthood and their sexual awakening are defined and exoticized against Victorian-era ideals and cultural norms. The two protagonists experience growth and find fulfillment, while the briefly featured indigenous people of the island play a frightening backdrop and an effective Other in the narrative against which their subjecthood is established. 

<div style={{ clear: "both" }} />

Nanuya Levu is now home to an eco-luxury resort. The film has produced a “paradise island” space that draws not only from its physical setting of the Turtle Island but also from the vast imaginary reservoir of the tropical Pacific; it now endures in the lasting trope of the desert island (with the incestuous romance and coming-of-age tale, unadulterated by rigid social norms, of course), and the eco-luxury hotel that will “serve as an idyllic escape for travellers seeking culture, community” and hosts only 14 couples at a time to “greet each day on [their] own stretch of pristine beach” (from the Turtle Island resort website).   

The direct environmental impact of the shoot is claimed to have been minimal, although one hears interesting details: for example, the island’s vegetation had to be spray painted green, since the shoot took place in Winter and the leaves had turned brown. (The island is privately owned; the island was already home to nonnative plants like pine trees, planted by its Oregonian owner).
